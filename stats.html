§ 1Instruction complexity

PA effect null · IA effect significant
Instruction complexity (English word count, n = 597) has no significant effect on PA judging (r = 0.047, p = 0.25) but significantly improves IA judging (r = −0.10, p = 0.015). Judges are uniformly weak at physics regardless of task complexity — they cannot even resolve which tasks are physically harder to assess. Instruction alignment behaves oppositely: longer prompts carry explicit, checkable sub-steps and are easier to score.

The IA effect survives control for generation quality (partial r = −0.098 vs raw r = −0.099) and therefore reflects the instruction itself, not the correlated video-quality difference. Partial-correlation detail in App. C.i.

§ 2Judge scores regress to the mean, compressing the top of the leaderboard

top-of-leaderboard resolution loss · not a judge-specific defect
With judge–human correlation below 1, scores show central-tendency regression: best clips are under-scored, worst clips are over-scored. Bias(judge − human) decreases monotonically with human gold across all 8 judge × dimension pairs; on PA, flash goes from +0.60 at human = 1 to −1.14 at human = 5; gemini-2.5-pro from +0.07 to −3.21.

We confirm this is regression to the mean — every judge's per-quality bias lies within ±0.15 of its own regression baseline E[J|H] = μJ + r · (σJ/σH)(H − μH).

This resolution loss falls exactly where it matters most. In human gold PA, the top-4 generators are nearly tied — a span of just 0.195, versus a full 2.177 from worst to best across all ten. For judges that mis-rank models, the errors concentrate at this compressed top: gemini-3.1-pro's six rank inversions all involve a top-4 model and gemini-3.5-flash places three of its four there, so the top-4 — only 13% of the 45 model pairs — absorbs roughly 67% of these judges' inversions. This is a consequence of regression to the mean, not a judge-specific defect: once the scale is compressed, the genuinely near-tied top is the first place ranking fidelity breaks down.

§ 2b"Judges the videos well" and "ranks the models correctly" are two different things

existence proof: gemini-2.5-pro PA-Pearson 0.24 vs τ_ap 0.928
One-line take. "Does the judge score each clip like a human (PA-Pearson)?" and "does the judge rank the 10 generator systems like a human (τ_ap)?" are two independent capabilities. The proof is that a judge exists whose two answers diverge: gemini-2.5-pro scores PA-Pearson 0.24 (near-random on individual clips) but PA-τ_ap 0.928 (best in the field) — one judge that judges clips badly yet almost perfectly reproduces the human generator ranking is enough to show the two capabilities can decouple. Table 1 reports both columns; the reader picks by use case.

Two questions, one table.
  • Q1 · Does the judge score each clip like a human? Read PA-Pearson. Best = gemini-3.5-flash 0.53; middle = seed2.0 / seed2.1 ≈ 0.50, gpt-5.x ≈ 0.42; worst = gemini-2.5-pro 0.24, qwen3-vl-8b 0.24, cosmos-reason2-2b 0.07 (near random).
  • Q2 · Does the judge rank the 10 generators like a human? Read τ_ap (top-weighted Kendall τ on the model-mean ranking). Best = gemini-2.5-pro 0.928, seed2.1 0.881, qwen3-vl-8b 0.807; middle = gemini-3.5-flash 0.733, seed2.0_pro 0.706; worst = cosmos family ≈ 0.39-0.44, gpt-5.x ≈ 0.49.
Why the two answers disagree. The ten generators' human-gold PA spans 1.86 – 4.04 (spread 2.18) — the ranking task itself is easy: a per-item-noisy judge that just keeps "good models > bad models" still ranks correctly, which is exactly what gemini-2.5-pro exploits. IA shows the same pattern. On the 10-judge base the population ρ(PA-Pearson, IA-τ_ap) is +0.81 (they travel together), but the moment cosmos-reason is added the ρ drops to +0.49 — because cosmos-reason2-32b is another anchoring judge on IA (Pearson only 0.377 but τ_ap 0.972). So the earlier "PA decouples, IA aligns" reading no longer holds; the correct read is "both axes carry anchoring judges, and population ρ drifts with the judge pool".

How to read anchoring judges correctly. On PA, gemini-2.5-pro is the clean anchoring existence proof (Pearson collapses, τ_ap preserved). On IA, cosmos-reason2-32b is the analogous case. But the cosmos-reason family is not a full mirror of gemini-2.5-pro — all three cosmos judges sit at PA-τ_ap 0.39-0.44 (well below the 19-judge median 0.485), i.e. in the low-P low-τap quadrant (bad per-item, bad at ranking).

VideoCon — the cleanest counter-example (single-variable control proving "anchor direction ≠ τ_ap"). VideoCon (a specialised video-text alignment scorer, not a general MLLM judge) and gemini-2.5-pro both anchor low on PA and both score near-randomly per item, yet their ranking behaviour is opposite:
  • gemini-2.5-pro: anchors "1" (strict) · PA-Pearson 0.24 (near random) · PA-τ_ap 0.928 (best in field)
  • VideoCon: anchors low (mean 1.51) · PA-Pearson 0.06 (near random) · PA-τ_ap 0.116 (16th of 18, IA-τ_ap −0.427 = worse than random)
The two share anchor direction and per-item randomness; the only variable that differs is τ_ap (0.928 vs 0.116). This is a single-variable control: holding "low anchor + per-item random" fixed, τ_ap still swings from 0.928 to 0.116 → what decides whether the ranking survives is not the anchor direction and not per-item accuracy, but whether the residual score beyond the anchor still monotonically recovers the gold ranking. gemini-2.5-pro's residual order is preserved; VideoCon's is not. Cleaner than the earlier cosmos-2b vs 2.5-pro pair, which differed on two variables (anchor direction and τ_ap); VideoCon differs on τ_ap only — a textbook counter-example.

The signature anchoring reversals remain gemini-2.5-pro (PA · τ_ap 0.928) + cosmos-reason2-32b (IA · τ_ap 0.972). Caveat: after the 18-judge median dropped (PA-τ_ap 0.567 → 0.493), internvl3.5-38b (PA · τ_ap 0.542, only marginally above the median) and cosmos-reason2-2b (IA · τ_ap 0.657) technically slip into the anchoring quadrant — but this is a median-shift artefact (their absolute τ_ap did not move; both remain well below the signature judges), not new evidence. VideoCon is still the cleanest side-by-side counter-example.

What Table 1 readers should do. Want a leaderboard-reproducing judge on the cheap → sort by τ_ap; gemini-2.5-pro alone is enough. Want a judge whose per-clip score you trust as a proxy for human judgement → read PA-Pearson; only gemini-3.5-flash / seed2.0 / seed2.1 clear the bar. Table 1 reports both columns because they measure independent capabilities, not two names for the same thing. Appendix G adds a third, non-ranking calibration distance (per-model RMSE against human means) that aligns with PA-Pearson at r = 0.84.

§ 2cJudges confabulate task success

PA 7.4 % ≈ IA 7.9 % · not instruction-conditioned · not random
On clips humans rate as clear failures, judges do not just miss the error — they confidently narrate a specific success. Independent judges collectively pass 7.9 % of IA violations and 7.4 % of PA violations; the rate is nearly identical, so the failure is not driven by the instruction (physical-adherence judging sees no instruction and shows the same rate). Aggregate leaderboard scores hide this — it only surfaces on the subset humans marked as clear violations.

§ 3Per-embodiment breakdown

generation quality varies · judge accuracy comparable
Generation quality varies sharply across end-effector categories: human hand 3.39 > gripper 2.98 > dexterous hand 2.93 (gold PA). AI-generated human-hand videos are the most physically realistic; dexterous-hand videos remain the hardest to generate.

Judge accuracy, however, shows no robust cross-embodiment pattern — Pearson orders (gripper 0.55 > dexterous 0.51 > human 0.49) and MAE orders (human 0.93 ≈ gripper 1.05 ≈ dexterous 1.04) disagree on which category is best-judged. We report the honest conclusion that judge accuracy is comparable across embodiments. The generation-quality-vs-judge-error contrast below makes this direct: green (generation quality) varies clearly while blue (judge MAE) is flat, so the residual per-embodiment differences are a confound of the true-score distribution, not judge competence. Statistical partial-correlation control in App. C.

Static view of the § 3 compression story; does not carry a headline claim.

Why the same judge can carry different labels across the two figures. Appendix D classifies by mean-shift + variance (σ-ratio, 7 categories); Appendix A flags variance collapse when a single bin exceeds 90 %. Both encode one mechanism: mass concentrates on an extreme bin, but whether residual spread survives determines ranking utility (§ 2b). gemini-2.5-pro anchors low (judge = 1 on 80.7 % of clips) yet retains residual spread (σ-ratio 0.84, not collapsed) → ranking is stable (τ_ap 0.928, CI [0.30, 0.93] excludes 0, the § 2b existence proof); robobrain / cosmos-reason2-2b / internvl3.5-8b / internvl3.5-38b instead collapse with no residual (σ-ratio 0.19 – 0.45) → ranking is fragile (all four τ_ap CIs include 0, so even where the point estimate is higher — e.g. internvl-38b 0.54 — ranking is not stable). "Anchored-low but not collapsed" (residual present) and "variance collapse" (no residual) are distinct outcomes, not conflicting labels.

CPartial-correlation control · Instruction complexity vs judge error

supporting § 1 · reviewer-defense statistical panel
Partial correlation of (word count, flash judge |error|) with gold score partialed out. If the effect survives the control (partial r ≈ raw r), the finding reflects instruction complexity itself; if it collapses, the raw effect was driven by the correlated video-quality difference. EVideo-generation model ranking

not prompt-controlled · appendix per § 3 caveat
Caveat. The current video-generation ranking is not prompt-controlled — each model was scored on a different subset of prompts drawn from the 875-item pool. In the meantime the ranking below serves as a reference exhibit only; do not read fine differences as model quality.

Because the top four generators are statistically tied in human gold PA (a 0.195 band on the 875-item set), the prompt-controlled common-set-50 evaluation is designed to resolve whether the top models are separable at all rather than to crown a winner: at n = 50 the paired bootstrap Δ CIs are expected to include zero for the top-four pairs, giving an honest "top-tier statistically tied" verdict. Cleanly separating the top would require a larger fair set or a more sensitive metric.

GPer-model calibration RMSE · complementary to τ_ap

non-rank distance metric · aligns with per-item Pearson
A non-rank complement to the τ_ap column in Table 1. For each judge we compute the mean judge score per generator and the mean human score per generator (10 base models, prefix / rewrite variants merged), then report the RMSE between the two 10-vectors. Because the metric preserves score magnitudes, anchoring judges — whose per-model means are compressed toward a single extreme — are penalized: gemini-2.5-pro's PA-RMSE is 1.81 and qwen-3-vl-8b's is 1.43, while seed2.0_pro sits at 0.19. This recovers alignment with per-item PA-Pearson (r = 0.84) that no purely rank-based metric achieves on this dataset. Reported alongside τ_ap so readers can see the ranking-versus-calibration axes side by side; system-level RMSE / MAE are standard model-level judge meta-evaluation metrics (see Chiang & Lee 2023; Zheng et al. 2023).
